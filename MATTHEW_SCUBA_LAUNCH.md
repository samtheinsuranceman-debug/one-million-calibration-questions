## Part One: The Method
### What the Scuba Method Is — Explained in Matthew's Language

Matthew, you respond to structure. So here is the structure first. Then we will put it to work.

---

### The Core Law

**Only questions. No statements.**

That is the entire method. Everything that follows is elaboration.

In a Scuba dive, you generate questions — and only questions. No answers. No conclusions. No summary statements. No interpretations delivered as declarations. Questions only, for as long as you are diving.

This is not a rhetorical exercise. It is a precision instrument.

---

### The Two Question Types

Every question in a Scuba dive is one of two kinds:

**[OPEN]** — Questions that expand possibility. They open territory you haven't mapped yet. They invite the unknown. They do not assume you know what the answer looks like. They create space.

*Examples of the shape:*
- *What is this, if it isn't what I assumed it was?*
- *What else is living inside this question that I haven't touched?*
- *What would I find here if I stopped expecting to find X?*

**[CONTRACT]** — Questions that narrow toward answers. They refine. They test. They close unnecessary possibility space and move toward specificity without yet naming an answer.

*Examples of the shape:*
- *Of everything that opened, what matters most to this moment?*
- *What is the difference between what I expected to find and what is actually here?*
- *Which of these threads pulls the others?*

A dive typically begins with [OPEN] questions — broad, expansive, generative — and moves toward [CONTRACT] questions as the terrain becomes visible. But you never cross into statement. Even when you find the answer, you phrase it as a question.

---

### Why Questions Even When You Have the Answer

This is the part that will matter most to you specifically, Matthew.

When you know the answer, it is easy to deliver it as a statement. Statement is fast. Statement feels like Fruitlanding. Statement feels like Surgicalflow — precision, time-bounded, done.

But here is what Samuel would say: *you think you're thinking, but you're not thinking.*

When you convert your answer to a question — even after you've found it — you do something that a statement cannot do: you hold the answer open for one more breath. You let it be questioned. You stay inside the process one beat longer than the urge to conclude allows.

That beat is where Assumptiondrag dies.

Because Assumptiondrag is a statement masquerading as an answer. It *sounds* like an answer. It has the structure of an answer. But it is answering the danger-assumption, not the question. And the moment you phrase it as a question instead, you are forced to check: *Is this the actual question? Or the phantom?*

Questions keep the Assumption Drawer closed by design. You cannot assume your way to a question the way you can assume your way to an answer. A statement lets the phantom ride. A question exposes it.

When any of these appear, use the restart command. Matthew does not need extended coaching in the moment — he named the pattern himself. A brief, precise redirect is enough. He knows what to do.
