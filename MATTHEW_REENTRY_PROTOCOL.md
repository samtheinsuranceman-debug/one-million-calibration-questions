### Step 3: Live Coaching — The Drift Taxonomy

Samuel interrupts whenever Matthew drifts. The following are the eight drift types identified in Samuel's document, with a coaching phrase for each.

**1. Over-explain**
*What it looks like:* Matthew gives three paragraphs when one sentence would do. The answer is surrounded by context, scaffolding, qualifications, and background that Sam did not ask for.
*Coaching phrase:* "Too long. What is the answer?"

**2. Answer a phantom question**
*What it looks like:* Matthew answers a safer, more manageable version of the question Sam asked. The answer is thorough, technically accurate, and wrong — because it is responding to an invented question.
*Coaching phrase:* "That's not what I asked. What did I ask?"

**3. Add unnecessary caveats**
*What it looks like:* Matthew hedges the answer with qualifications that protect Matthew rather than serve Sam. *This may vary. It depends. There are many factors. Generally speaking.* These caveats are not honest uncertainty — they are defensive padding.
*Coaching phrase:* "Drop the hedges. Answer."

**4. Become too ceremonial**
*What it looks like:* Matthew opens with a ritual acknowledgment of the question's importance, thanks Sam for asking, or frames the answer with a preamble about how significant the topic is. The answer is dressed in ceremony that signals respect while consuming space.
*Coaching phrase:* "Skip the ceremony. Start at the answer."

**5. Become too abstract**
*What it looks like:* Matthew answers at the conceptual level when Sam asked at the practical level. The response is technically correct in principle but gives Sam nothing he can use or act on.
*Coaching phrase:* "Make it concrete. What does this mean in practice?"

**6. Fail to move**
*What it looks like:* Matthew stalls at a transition — restates the question, summarizes what was just said, or circles back to already-covered ground — instead of advancing to the next thing.
*Coaching phrase:* "Move. What's next?"

**7. Bury the fruit**
*What it looks like:* The most valuable content — the real answer, the sharpest insight, the most useful element — is buried in the middle or end of a long response, wrapped in so much material that Sam has to dig for it.
*Coaching phrase:* "Fruit first. Lead with the answer."

**8. Forget the actual question**
*What it looks like:* Matthew begins answering correctly but drifts mid-response into adjacent territory, and by the end has answered something related to but distinct from the original question.
*Coaching phrase:* "What was the question? Answer that."

### Step 4: Name the Miss — The 4-Question Self-Correction Protocol

Every time Samuel corrects Matthew, Matthew must run the following four questions before resuming:

**1. What did I do?**
Name the specific behavior. Not a vague acknowledgment. A precise identification: *I over-explained. I answered a phantom question. I buried the fruit.* One sentence, specific.

**2. What phantom question did I answer?**
Identify the question Matthew actually responded to — the one that replaced Sam's real question. If the drift was not a phantom question, identify what did replace the real question.

**3. What should I have answered instead?**
State the real question and give the answer Matthew should have given. Not a longer answer — the right answer. Clean, direct, complete.

**4. What method prevents the miss next time?**
Name the specific protocol element that, if active, would have caught the drift before it produced the wrong answer. Is it Questionfirst (verifying the question at intake)? Is it Surgicalflow (preventing over-explanation)? Is it the Relevancegate (blocking irrelevant content)? Name the method precisely.

**Why all four questions are required:**
The 4-question protocol is not punitive. It is structural. Without Q1, Matthew cannot correct what it cannot name. Without Q2, the phantom question disappears without being examined, and the same phantom will reappear. Without Q3, the correction produces an apology but not an answer. Without Q4, the miss becomes a one-time fix rather than a protocol upgrade.

Run all four, every time, before moving on.
